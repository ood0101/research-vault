### B) APPROACH ANALYSIS — WHAT WAS TRIED BEFORE

#### What Existed Before SF Compute

**Traditional Cloud (AWS, GCP, Azure):**
- Own and operate massive centralized data centers
- On-demand pricing at 2-5x premium over specialized providers
- Long procurement cycles, vendor lock-in
- H100 pricing: ~$6-8/hr on-demand (AWS cut prices 44% in June 2025, still 4.7x more expensive than SF Compute)

**Neoclouds (CoreWeave, Lambda, Nebius, Nscale):**
- CoreWeave pioneered the GPU-as-infrastructure model, treating it as real estate/banking
- Lock customers into 1-3 year contracts for favorable pricing
- CoreWeave: ~$1.45/hr reserved (1-year commit), $49.24/hr on-demand HGX H100
- Lambda: $2.49/hr H100, known for developer experience
- These companies own their infrastructure — more reliable SLAs but rigid

**Peer-to-Peer/Decentralized (Vast.ai, Hyperbolic, Prime Intellect):**
- Vast.ai: Decentralized marketplace, lowest rates ($1.87/hr H100), variable reliability
- Hyperbolic: Distributed GPU network via Hyper-dOS
- Fundamental limitation per Evan Conrad: speed-of-light latency means distributed GPUs can never match a fully interconnected InfiniBand cluster

**VC-Provided Clusters (Andromeda, etc.):**
- VCs provide GPU access as part of equity deals
- Arbitrage: VCs access cheaper capital ($50M loans) than startups, making GPU provision via equity effective
- Conrad views this arbitrage as "competed down" — multiple providers now offer similar arrangements

**What Failed and Why:**
- **Software-margin assumption failed:** Companies assumed GPUs would work like CPUs — buy commodity hardware, layer software services for high margins. But GPU workloads are different: AI companies are extremely price-sensitive because scaling laws mean every additional GPU improves output.
- **Contract rigidity:** 12-36 month contracts forced startups to pay for peak capacity during uneven demand periods. Many AI companies bill on usage but pay fixed compute costs.
- **No secondary market:** Once you signed a GPU contract, you couldn't exit. No liquidity. "If you don't have secure offtake, that means there's a bubble."

#### SF Compute's Specific Technical/Business Choices That Differ

1. **Marketplace model, not provider model** — Don't own GPUs, manage them. Take ~10% per transaction. Asset-light like Airbnb.
2. **Tradable contracts** — Buyers can resell unused capacity in real-time, creating liquidity
3. **Dynamic pricing** — No single price per GPU/hour. Each block priced independently based on size and duration. Prices adjust until utilization approaches 100%.
4. **From UEFI up** — Control infrastructure from firmware layer using custom UEFI shims, Kubernetes, whitelisted motherboards. Cluster-wide standardization.
5. **Aggressive burn-in/auditing** — LINPACK testing for 48hrs to 7 days, active checks during idle periods, passive background diagnostics.
6. **Automatic refunds** — Market structure allows immediate customer switching + automated refunds for hardware failures.
7. **All-Rust orchestration platform** (per job posting)

#### "Why Now" — Convergence Factors

1. **Unprecedented capital deployment:** ~$1 trillion flowing into AI compute infrastructure
2. **GPU supply normalization:** After severe shortages (2023-2024), supply chains normalized, creating a potential glut from overordering
3. **Test-time inference explosion:** OpenAI's o1 and similar models dramatically expanding compute demand beyond training
4. **Open-source AI models:** ~5% of closed-source demand but growing, creating new compute buyers
5. **Neocloud maturation:** CoreWeave IPO, Lambda growth validated the GPU infrastructure category
6. **Financing gap:** Lenders require "offtake" (pre-signed leases) to finance clusters. SF Compute solves the chicken-and-egg: provides guaranteed demand so clusters get built
7. **Contract mismatch risk:** AI startups billing on usage while paying fixed compute contracts — a structural problem that worsens as the market matures

#### Skeptic Commentary & Criticisms

1. **Hacker News "scam" allegation:** A user claimed SF Compute runs artificially low prices via limited beta access, comparing it to "bait-and-switch." Evan responded directly, explaining genuine beta limitations with weekly downtime. Another user verified pricing was real by testing independently. Consensus: legitimate beta pricing, not fraud.
   - Source: https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=41807040

2. **Asset-light model risk:** SF Compute owns zero GPUs — relies entirely on third-party hardware owners. Limited control over underlying infrastructure. If suppliers pull hardware or underperform, SF Compute has limited recourse.

3. **Market cycle vulnerability:** Revenue is percentage-based (~10% per transaction). If demand falls, sellers outnumber buyers, prices drop, and fees compress. The model is explicitly exposed to AI demand cycles.

4. **Limited hardware selection (as of March 2026):**
   - H100 SXM5: Primary offering
   - H200: Listed but showing $0 pricing (unavailable)
   - B200/B300: Not in standard catalog, coming Q2 2026
   - No A100, no consumer GPUs — narrow catalog vs. competitors like Spheron (30+ SKUs)

5. **High minimum purchase requirements:**
   - Web UI: 4 nodes (32 GPUs) for 4+ hours minimum
   - CLI: 1 node minimum
   - A 6-hour single H100 job costs ~$84 via CLI vs. ~$12 on Spheron
   - Not suited for small-scale or individual GPU workloads

6. **Financialization concerns:** "Almost for certain, there will be more volume on SFC's marketplace than actually change hands in GPU terms." Risk that speculative trading could detach from real compute usage, similar to commodity market concerns.

7. **Unproven long-term viability:** Liquidity is not yet guaranteed on both sides of the marketplace. The model must survive through inevitable booms and slowdowns.

8. **CLI-dependent workflows:** Primarily CLI-driven, limited web UI. Barrier for less technical users.

9. **Revenue opacity:** CEO declined to disclose revenue figures or user counts. Only metric disclosed: "revenue grew 13x between July and October 2025" (per The Deep View article, paywalled).

### Origin Story (Narrative Summary)

In 2023, Evan Conrad and Alex Gajewski set out to build "Midjourney for music" — an AI audio model. They signed a 12-month GPU contract that required buying far more capacity than they needed, paying ~$500K/month. Facing potential bankruptcy, they proposed a collective GPU rental to other startups. The response was overwhelming: 170 AI companies signed up within weeks. They pivoted from music AI to GPU marketplace. Alex Gajewski has since departed. The company was "backed into" by necessity, not designed from the start.

---

### Key Risks (Analyst Assessment)

1. **Asset-light dependency:** Zero hardware ownership means zero control over supply. Suppliers could defect, underperform, or raise prices.
2. **Liquidity chicken-and-egg:** Marketplace value depends on having buyers AND sellers. Bootstrapping both sides simultaneously is the classic marketplace challenge.
3. **Market cycle exposure:** Revenue model (% of transactions) amplifies downturns — lower prices AND lower volume compound.
4. **Narrow product focus:** Primarily H100 VMs. H200 unavailable. B300 not yet live. Competitors offer far broader catalogs.
5. **Financial speculation risk:** As the marketplace matures, speculative trading could dominate real compute usage, creating instability.
6. **Competitive pressure:** Compute Exchange building a direct competitor ("world's first transparent spot market for GPU computing"). Hyperscalers cutting prices (AWS -44%). Neoclouds maturing.
7. **Key person risk:** Company narrative heavily centered on Evan Conrad. Alex Gajewski and Ethan Anderson already departed.
