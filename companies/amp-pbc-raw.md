### B) APPROACH ANALYSIS — WHAT WAS TRIED BEFORE

#### What existed before AMP:

1. **Hyperscaler clouds (AWS, GCP, Azure)** — Generic cloud providers that don't solve frontier workload problems (silent data corruption, topology-aware scheduling, power management, fast checkpointing). Expensive, not optimized for AI training.

2. **GPU-as-a-Service startups:**
   - **CoreWeave** — Specialized NVIDIA GPU cloud. Kubernetes-native. Strong for large-scale training. InfiniBand networking. H100s at ~$1.45/hr reserved. But complex (requires Kubernetes expertise).
   - **Lambda Labs** — Developer-friendly GPU cloud. 16,000+ organizations. Transparent pricing. H100 at $2.49/hr on-demand. Less orchestration than CoreWeave.
   - **Together AI** — Model-serving platform / hosted API for open-source models. Not raw GPU infrastructure.
   - **RunPod** — Serverless GPU. Fast provisioning (<15 sec). Good for inference.
   - **Vast.ai** — "Airbnb of GPUs." Marketplace model. Cheapest option (40-60% below on-demand).
   - **Modal** — Serverless GPU platform for generative AI.

3. **a16z Oxygen program** — Midha's own predecessor. $1.25B, 20,000+ GPUs. Bundled GPU access with venture investment. But tied to a16z portfolio companies only. Equity-for-compute model.

4. **hosted.ai** — "VMware for GPUs." $19M seed (Creandum). GPU virtualization for pooling, multi-tenancy, overcommit. Aimed at neoclouds/CSPs, not frontier AI labs.

**What failed / what's insufficient:**
- Generic clouds don't solve frontier-specific infrastructure problems
- Dedicated GPU clouds solve access but not utilization (each team still has spiky demand)
- Each independent lab rebuilds the same infra team (schedulers, ops, checkpointing) — massive duplication
- Oxygen was oversubscribed ("I can't allocate enough") and limited to a16z portfolio
- No existing solution addresses the pooling/smoothing of demand curves across independent teams

#### How the space evolved:
- **2022-2023:** GPU scarcity crisis. H100 supply crunch. AI startups couldn't get compute at any price. VCs (a16z) started acquiring GPUs directly.
- **2024:** GPU-as-a-service market exploded. CoreWeave valued at $19B+. Nvidia supply improved but demand grew faster. a16z Oxygen launched.
- **2025:** DeepSeek showed efficiency gains don't reduce overall GPU hunger — teams do more with each FLOP but want even more FLOPs. Nation-states began treating AI compute as sovereign infrastructure. Hyperscalers committed $100B+ to AI data centers.
- **2026:** The paradigm shift is from "access to GPUs" to "efficient utilization of GPUs at scale." The waste problem (30-40% idle FLOPs) becomes the central issue.

#### AMP's specific technical choices:
- **Grid model** (vs. cloud model): pools compute across providers, not a single provider
- **Public benefit corporation structure**: legally enshrines mission beyond profit
- **Member-owned baseload**: each member retains full control of their own baseload
- **Demand smoothing**: statistical multiplexing across teams in different phases
- **Shared automation**: solve infra problems (checkpointing, scheduling, corruption detection) once for all members
- **Cross-provider**: "pools compute across providers" — not locked to one hardware vendor

#### The "Why Now":
1. Number of frontier-capable teams is exploding (5-person labs in 2026 can do what large teams did in 2022)
2. AI tooling compounds returns to talent density and penalizes organizational bloat
3. GPU supply has improved but utilization remains terrible (30-40% waste)
4. Cost of frontier training runs is astronomical — waste at this scale is intolerable
5. Every independent lab is redundantly building the same infra team
6. The choice between independence and compute access has become existential
7. Midha has the network and credibility to assemble the coalition (board seats at most major independent labs)

### C) THIRD-PARTY & SKEPTIC COMMENTARY

#### Objections acknowledged in AMP's own thesis:
1. **"It's just a cloud with more steps"** — AMP's response: A grid pools compute across providers for maximum flexibility; a cloud is a single provider.
2. **"Members will graduate"** — AMP's response: Individual capabilities (scheduler, ops team) do get less valuable at scale, BUT collective capabilities (smoothed demand curve, collective intelligence, crisis security) get MORE valuable at scale.

#### Structural criticisms (from the digidai analysis):
- **Centralization paradox**: "By controlling access to scarce compute resources, Midha wields extraordinary power over which startups receive the infrastructure necessary to compete. This gatekeeping function creates structural dependencies."
- **Conflict of interest**: Midha retains a16z board seats while running AMP. Questions about whether AMP competes with Oxygen for deals and creates conflicting incentives for a16z portfolio companies.
- AMP is "marketed as democratizing initiatives, preventing AI development from becoming concentrated among a few large companies. But the reality inverts the rhetoric."

#### Technical risks of compute pooling / multi-tenancy:
1. **Noisy neighbor problem**: One tenant's workload degrades another's performance. Especially critical for low-latency inference and large training runs.
2. **Security / memory safety**: GPUs expose device memory. Running shared workloads risks data leakage between tenants.
3. **Isolation limitations**: GPU time-slicing shares memory with no per-pod restrictions. MIG only works on modern NVIDIA GPUs with inflexible predefined profiles.
4. **Workload contention**: Training and inference compete for the same headroom without QoS mechanisms.
5. **Rapid hardware depreciation**: H100s lose value quickly. Running at <40% utilization on depreciating hardware is a money-losing proposition.
6. **Cost attribution**: Difficulty metering and charging back GPU usage in shared environments.
7. **Coordination overhead**: Managing a multi-party grid with independent members who have competing priorities.

#### Broader skepticism about compute scaling:
- Alignment Forum: "Most researchers might be operating under an abundance of cheap compute... leading them to not invest the effort required to more efficiently optimize" — challenges the narrative that more compute is the answer.
- DeepSeek R1 showed significant efficiency gains with less compute, raising questions about whether scaling compute is truly the bottleneck vs. algorithmic innovation.
- Public opposition to AI infrastructure is heating up (TechCrunch, Feb 2026) — community resistance to data center builds.

#### The graduation risk in detail:
- As any grid member scales (e.g., Anthropic is already spending $28B/yr on compute), they can build their own infrastructure. The grid's value to the largest members may diminish over time.
- Counter: Anthropic, despite its scale, still uses external compute partners (Google TPUs, Microsoft Azure, Fluidstack). Even the largest players benefit from multi-provider flexibility.

### Product / Technology

**The Grid Concept:**
- Decouples two scaling problems: innovation scales through independence; compute scales through pooled infrastructure
- Statistical multiplexing: different members in different phases (training vs inference vs idle) smooth aggregate demand curve
- Each member retains full control of their own baseload
- Grid solves hard infra problems once: silent data corruption detection, topology-aware scheduling, power management, fast checkpointing
- "Pools compute across providers" — cross-provider, not locked to one cloud
- Eliminates duplicated effort (each lab currently builds own infra team for same problems)

**Utilization economics:**
- Currently 30-40% of FLOPs frequently unused within independent teams
- Teams feel perpetually under-resourced despite this waste
- Grid smooths demand: when one member trains, another deploys, another is idle
- Collective utilization target presumably 70%+ (the profitability threshold for GPU hardware)

**Infrastructure scale:**
- Targeting 1 GW data center capacity
- 1 GW = ~1 million US households' electrical demand
- At this scale, comparable to the largest hyperscaler deployments

### Why This Is a Non-Consensus Bet

**What the consensus thinks:**
1. AI compute is a cloud problem — hyperscalers and GPU clouds will serve everyone
2. The largest labs (OpenAI, Google, Anthropic) will vertically integrate and build their own massive data centers
3. Smaller labs will either join big companies or use commodity GPU clouds
4. Public benefit corporations are a branding exercise, not a meaningful structural constraint
5. Compute pooling introduces too many coordination and trust problems to work at frontier scale

**What AMP believes differently:**
1. Independence is a feature, not a bug — the most productive AI teams are small, focused, and independent
2. The current approach wastes 30-40% of the world's most expensive input (frontier compute)
3. A grid (not a cloud) can pool compute while preserving independence
4. The grid's value *increases* at scale (smoothed demand, collective intelligence, crisis resilience)
5. A PBC structure is necessary to align the grid operator's incentives with its members
6. The number of frontier-capable teams is *exploding*, not consolidating — creating a massive addressable market
7. The person best positioned to build this is someone with board seats at the actual member labs
